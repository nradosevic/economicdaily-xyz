5 Metrics VCs Are Using to Evaluate AI Startups Beyond Revenue

By Jonathan Park • March 4, 2026 • Startups

The venture capital playbook for evaluating software companies has undergone a significant revision as investors grapple with the unique characteristics of AI startups. Traditional metrics like annual recurring revenue, customer acquisition costs, and lifetime value remain important, but they tell an incomplete story for companies whose value proposition depends on proprietary data, model capabilities, and technical talent that don't easily fit into conventional frameworks. Leading AI-focused investors are developing new evaluation approaches that better capture the sources of competitive advantage in machine learning-driven businesses.

Data asset quality has emerged as perhaps the most critical differentiator for AI startups. Investors are looking beyond dataset size to assess the uniqueness, proprietary nature, and ongoing refresh rate of training data. Companies that have built data flywheels—where their product generates new data that continuously improves model performance—receive premium valuations because this dynamic creates compounding advantages over time. The key question investors ask is whether a startup's data moat will widen or narrow as the market matures.

Model performance benchmarks provide another crucial evaluation dimension, though interpreting them requires sophistication. Raw accuracy metrics on standard benchmarks may matter less than performance on domain-specific tasks relevant to target customers. Investors increasingly conduct technical due diligence that goes beyond accepting reported numbers to understand how models perform at the edges of their capabilities and how gracefully they fail. The ability to maintain performance as models are scaled down for efficient deployment also factors into evaluation, as compute costs significantly impact unit economics.

Research velocity and publication records offer signals about a company's technical capabilities and ability to attract top talent. Investors track not just the quantity of publications but their impact, as measured by citations and adoption of proposed methods. However, savvy investors also recognize that the best commercial AI research may not be published—some of the most valuable technical innovations are kept proprietary. The key is assessing whether a company has the research depth to stay ahead of rapidly advancing capabilities in the broader field.

Technical team composition and retention rates receive intense scrutiny in AI startup evaluation. Unlike traditional software where engineering talent is somewhat fungible, elite machine learning researchers and engineers are extraordinarily scarce and command significant compensation. Investors assess whether founding teams have the technical credibility to recruit top researchers and whether equity structures and company culture will support long-term retention. The departure of key technical personnel can severely impact an AI startup's trajectory, making team stability a significant risk factor.

Infrastructure efficiency and compute economics represent an increasingly important evaluation dimension. As AI models grow larger, the costs of training and serving them can become prohibitive. Startups that have developed more efficient architectures, better training methodologies, or proprietary optimization techniques can achieve superior unit economics compared to competitors. Investors evaluate whether companies have a path to profitable operations as they scale, or whether growing compute costs will consume the benefits of revenue growth.

These metrics are being synthesized into new valuation frameworks that attempt to capture AI-specific value creation. Some investors are developing "AI moat scores" that weight different factors based on the particular market a startup addresses. Others focus on the sustainability of competitive advantages, recognizing that the rapid pace of AI advancement can quickly erode technical moats. What unites these approaches is a recognition that evaluating AI companies requires looking beyond financial statements to understand the technical and data foundations that will determine long-term success in an intensely competitive and rapidly evolving landscape.
