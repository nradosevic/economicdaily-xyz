What CFOs Are Saying About AI Adoption: A Survey of Enterprise Priorities

By Katherine Wong • March 6, 2026 • Business

Chief financial officers at major enterprises are increasingly viewing artificial intelligence not as a speculative technology bet but as a core component of financial strategy. A comprehensive survey of 500 CFOs across multiple industries reveals a significant shift in attitudes over the past two years, with AI moving from experimental pilot programs to enterprise-wide deployment plans backed by substantial budgets. However, the survey also highlights persistent concerns about implementation challenges, talent shortages, and the difficulty of measuring return on investment for AI initiatives.

The survey found that 78% of CFOs now consider AI investment a top-three priority for technology spending, up from just 45% two years ago. This dramatic increase reflects growing confidence in AI's ability to deliver tangible business value, driven by high-profile success stories and the maturation of enterprise AI platforms. Finance leaders report particular enthusiasm for AI applications in predictive analytics, process automation, and customer service—areas where measurable efficiency gains can be demonstrated relatively quickly.

Budget allocations tell a compelling story about shifting priorities. The average enterprise AI budget has grown by 65% year-over-year, with the largest increases occurring in financial services, healthcare, and manufacturing. CFOs report that these investments are being funded through a combination of reallocated technology spending, new capital expenditure, and in some cases, dedicated transformation funds established specifically for AI initiatives. The willingness to commit significant resources signals a transition from cautious experimentation to confident scaling.

Despite the enthusiasm, CFOs identify several persistent challenges to successful AI implementation. Talent acquisition ranks as the top concern, with 67% of respondents reporting difficulty finding and retaining employees with the skills necessary to develop and deploy AI systems. Data quality and integration challenges follow closely behind, as many organizations struggle to consolidate the fragmented data assets necessary for effective machine learning. These operational challenges often prove more difficult to overcome than the technical hurdles of AI development itself.

Measuring the return on AI investment remains a significant challenge for finance leaders. While some applications—such as automation of routine financial processes—offer clear and quantifiable savings, the benefits of other AI initiatives are more diffuse or longer-term in nature. CFOs report developing new frameworks for evaluating AI investments that account for strategic value, competitive positioning, and capability building alongside traditional financial metrics. This evolution in measurement approaches reflects a maturing understanding of how AI creates value in enterprise contexts.

Risk management considerations are also shaping AI investment decisions. CFOs express concern about regulatory compliance, particularly in light of emerging AI governance frameworks in Europe and other jurisdictions. Reputational risks associated with AI failures or biased outcomes are weighing on investment decisions, leading some organizations to prioritize lower-risk applications while more carefully evaluating deployments in sensitive areas. The survey suggests that finance leaders are balancing competitive pressure to adopt AI quickly against prudent risk management.

Looking ahead, CFOs anticipate continued acceleration of AI investment through 2027 and beyond. The majority expect AI to fundamentally transform their organizations' competitive positioning within five years, creating urgency around current implementation efforts. However, finance leaders also emphasize the importance of building sustainable AI capabilities rather than pursuing short-term gains that may prove difficult to maintain. The most sophisticated organizations are treating AI adoption as a multi-year transformation journey requiring sustained investment in technology, talent, and organizational change.
