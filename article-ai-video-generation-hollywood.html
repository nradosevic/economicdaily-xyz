AI Video Generation Reaches Hollywood Quality: What It Means for the Entertainment Industry

By Miranda Brooks • February 25, 2026 • Lifestyle

When OpenAI released Sora in early 2024, the generated videos were impressive but clearly artificial—physics behaved strangely, backgrounds shifted inconsistently, and human movements had an uncanny quality. Two years later, the latest generation of AI video tools has eliminated most of these tells. Industry insiders report that production companies are now using AI-generated footage in commercial projects, and audiences cannot reliably distinguish synthetic content from traditionally filmed material. The entertainment industry is grappling with what this means for everything from production budgets to labor relations to the nature of creative work itself.

The economics of video production are being upended. A thirty-second commercial that once required location scouts, camera crews, lighting technicians, and days of shooting can now be generated from a text description in hours. Advertising agencies report using AI tools to produce dozens of variations for A/B testing at a fraction of historical costs. For brands with limited budgets, this democratization opens possibilities that were previously financially impossible—television-quality commercials for small businesses, personalized video content at scale, rapid iteration on creative concepts.

Film and television production is incorporating AI tools more cautiously but inexorably. Visual effects houses are using generative models to create background environments, extend sets digitally, and produce establishing shots that would be expensive or impossible to film traditionally. Some production companies are experimenting with AI-generated b-roll and stock footage. The most adventurous are using these tools to visualize entire sequences during pre-production, enabling directors to make creative decisions before a single frame is shot traditionally.

The implications for creative professionals are complex and contested. Unions representing actors, writers, and production workers have raised alarms about AI-generated content replacing human labor. The SAG-AFTRA strike of 2023 centered partly on AI concerns, and contract negotiations across the entertainment industry now routinely address how these technologies can and cannot be used. Yet some creators embrace AI as a tool that expands their capabilities, enabling individual artists to produce content that previously required large teams.

Legal and ethical questions abound. Who owns the rights to AI-generated content? What happens when these systems produce footage that closely resembles copyrighted material or features likenesses of real people? Several high-profile lawsuits are working through courts, with outcomes that could reshape the legal landscape around synthetic media. Meanwhile, deepfake concerns have intensified as the technology to create convincing fake videos of real people becomes trivially accessible.

The authenticity premium may become entertainment's next frontier. As AI-generated content becomes ubiquitous, there's growing interest in content that is verifiably human-created, traditionally produced, or "authentic" in some demonstrable way. Some platforms are implementing provenance tracking to distinguish AI-generated material. Others are marketing the human origins of their content as a feature rather than an assumption. Whether audiences ultimately care about this distinction remains to be seen.

Perhaps most profoundly, AI video generation raises questions about the nature of filmmaking as an art form. If a director can describe a scene in words and have it rendered photographically, what becomes of cinematography, set design, and the dozens of craft disciplines that have defined the medium? The optimistic view holds that these tools will democratize storytelling, enabling more diverse voices to bring their visions to screen. The pessimistic view suggests a future of homogenized, algorithmically optimized content. The reality will likely fall somewhere between these extremes, and the entertainment industry is only beginning to navigate this transformation.
